## Interoception /ˌɪn.tər.oʊˈsɛp.ʃən/

**Part of speech:** noun

**Etymology:** From Latin *inter-* (within, interior) + *receptio* (a receiving, from *recipere*, to receive). The term was coined by British physiologist and Nobel laureate Sir Charles Sherrington around 1906 in his landmark work *The Integrative Action of the Nervous System*, to describe the nervous system's perception of the body's internal environment. First recorded in English: c. 1906.

---

### Definitions

1. *(neuroscience / physiology)* The sensory system by which the brain perceives and integrates signals from the body's internal organs — including the heart, lungs, stomach, gut, and skin — encompassing sensations of hunger, thirst, pain, temperature, heartbeat, breathlessness, and the physiological substates that underlie emotion.

2. *(psychology / cognitive science)* The broader capacity to be consciously aware of, and accurately interpret, one's own internal bodily states — widely regarded as a foundational component of emotional intelligence, self-regulation, and embodied cognition.

3. *(clinical / therapeutic usage)* The cultivated or therapeutically developed attunement to internal physical signals, as practised in somatic therapies, mindfulness-based interventions, and trauma-informed care — often described as "listening to the body."

### Why This Word Is Everywhere Right Now

Once a term confined to neurophysiology textbooks, *interoception* has broken into the mainstream of mental health discourse, self-improvement culture, and clinical therapy — and by 2026, it stands as one of the most frequently invoked concepts in conversations about emotional regulation, neurodivergence, and psychological healing.

The somatic therapy movement deserves much of the credit. Modalities such as Somatic Experiencing, EMDR, and Internal Family Systems have surged in popularity, and each places bodily awareness at the centre of healing. Clinicians across these traditions have become vocal about a simple but counterintuitive proposition: you cannot talk your way out of a trauma response that lives in the body. To heal emotionally, a person must first be able to *feel* internally — and that capacity has a name. As these therapies have migrated from specialist clinics to mainstream podcasts and social media, so has the vocabulary.

Neuroscience has simultaneously delivered a flood of research that reframes how emotion itself works. The predictive processing model of the brain — popularised by neuroscientist Lisa Feldman Barrett in her influential work *How Emotions Are Made* — argues that emotions are not simply reactions to the world but are largely the brain's predictions built upon interoceptive signals. Feeling "anxious," on this account, may partly be the brain's interpretation of a rapid heartbeat and shallow breath rather than a direct readout of the environment. The implication is striking: if interoception is the raw data of emotional experience, then training interoception becomes a concrete path to reshaping how emotion is constructed.

The neurodivergence conversation has added another layer of urgency to the term. Research increasingly shows that many autistic and ADHD individuals have atypical interoceptive profiles — marked difficulty in sensing hunger, thirst, fatigue, or pain — which explains a wide range of challenges that had previously been attributed to motivation or behaviour alone. As neurodiversity awareness and self-identification continue to grow, so does the vocabulary people reach for to make sense of their inner lives.

### Related Words

- **Proprioception** — the sense of the body's position and movement in space; often discussed alongside interoception as the two primary forms of body-sense
- **Exteroception** — perception of stimuli originating outside the body, forming the complementary contrast to interoception
- **Alexithymia** — a condition marked by difficulty identifying and articulating one's own emotions, now frequently linked to atypical interoception
- **Allostasis** — the process by which the body maintains stability by anticipating and adapting to change, the physiological backdrop against which interoception operates
- **Embodied cognition** — the theoretical framework positing that thought and emotion are grounded in bodily states, of which interoception is a primary mechanism
