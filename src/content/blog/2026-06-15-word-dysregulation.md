## Dysregulation /dɪsˌrɛɡjʊˈleɪʃən/

**Part of speech:** noun

**Etymology:** From the Greek prefix *dys-* ("bad, difficult, impaired") joined to *regulation*, itself from the Latin *regula* ("a rule, a straight stick"). The compound entered clinical and physiological literature in the mid-twentieth century (first attested in this sense around 1935) to describe a breakdown in the body's self-correcting systems.

---

### Definitions

1. *(physiology)* The failure of a biological system to maintain its normal regulatory balance; impairment of a process that ordinarily keeps a variable within a healthy range, as in the dysregulation of blood sugar or body temperature.
2. *(psychology)* An impaired ability to manage and modulate one's emotional responses, such that feelings arrive too intensely, last too long, or shift too abruptly to be steadied by ordinary means; most often encountered in the phrase *emotional dysregulation*.
3. *(informal / colloquial usage)* A catch-all term for feeling internally "off" — keyed up, frazzled, or unable to calm down — borrowed from the therapist's vocabulary into everyday speech.

---

### Usage in a Sentence

> "The clinician explained that his outbursts were not defiance but emotional dysregulation, a nervous system overwhelmed faster than it could recover."

> "After three back-to-back meetings and no lunch, I was in full dysregulation by mid-afternoon and snapped at an email that didn't deserve it."

---

### Why This Word Is Everywhere Right Now

For most of its life *dysregulation* lived quietly in medical journals and the diagnostic manuals of psychiatry, a precise term for systems — endocrine, autonomic, affective — that had lost their grip on equilibrium. In the last few years it has escaped the clinic. Scroll through any mental-health corner of social media and you will find people describing themselves as "dysregulated" the way an earlier generation might have said they were stressed, frazzled, or simply having a bad day. The word has become the default vocabulary for a culture increasingly fluent in the language of the nervous system.

Part of the appeal is that *dysregulation* feels both scientific and forgiving. It locates distress in the body's machinery rather than in a person's character: you are not weak or dramatic, your regulatory system is momentarily overwhelmed. That framing dovetails with the wider popularization of polyvagal theory, "nervous system regulation" influencers, and the booming market for breathwork, cold plunges, and vagus-nerve gadgets — all of them promising to return a jangled body to baseline. Where therapy once spoke of feelings, the new wellness dialect speaks of states, set points, and getting "back into the window of tolerance."

But the word's migration carries the usual risks of clinical language gone viral. Stretched to cover every flash of irritation or restlessness, *dysregulation* can quietly pathologize the ordinary texture of being alive — hunger, fatigue, frustration, grief. Clinicians note that not all difficult emotion is dysregulation; sometimes a strong feeling is simply the correct response to circumstances. The term's rise is a useful mirror: it shows a society that has learned to take its inner weather seriously, while also revealing how readily we now reach for a diagnosis to name what used to be called, plainly, a hard day.

---

### Related Words

- **regulation** — the steadying counterpart; the capacity to return a system, or a self, to balance.
- **homeostasis** — the body's drive to hold its internal conditions stable, the very equilibrium that dysregulation disrupts.
- **allostasis** — stability achieved through change rather than constancy; the cost of constant adjustment can itself tip into dysregulation.
- **overwhelm** — the everyday word for the felt experience that *dysregulation* dresses in clinical clothing.
