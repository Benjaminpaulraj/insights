## Delulu /dɪˈluːluː/

**Part of speech:** adjective (informal); occasionally noun

**Etymology:** A clipped and reduplicated form of *delusional*, itself from the Latin *dēlūdere* ("to deceive, to play false"), via *dēlūsiō*. The slang contraction emerged within English-speaking K-pop fan communities around 2013–2014 — originally describing fans with improbable romantic hopes about idols — before crossing into mainstream usage between 2023 and 2025. Formally recognized by the Cambridge Dictionary in 2025.

---

### Definitions

1. *(primary meaning)* Believing things that are not realistic or true, typically because one wishes them to be so; holding to a hope or self-image in defiance of evidence.
2. *(colloquial, often approving)* Possessing an exaggerated, almost willful confidence in a favourable outcome — used as a kind of motivational stance rather than a criticism.
3. *(as noun)* A person who is delulu; one given to flattering self-delusion.

---

### Usage in a Sentence

> "He's convinced the promotion is his despite three more qualified candidates — completely delulu."

> "Her mantra for the year was 'delulu is the solulu' — that a little irrational self-belief was the only way she'd ever take the leap."

---

### Why This Word Is Everywhere Right Now

What makes *delulu* remarkable is not merely that it migrated from niche fandom to the dictionary, but that it inverted its own meaning along the way. *Delusional* is, in clinical and everyday speech, a hard word — a diagnosis, an insult, a warning. *Delulu* is soft, even affectionate. Stripped of its medical weight and softened by reduplication's nursery-rhyme cadence, it became something you could call yourself with a grin. The companion phrase "delulu is the solulu" (delusion is the solution) turned the term into a half-ironic life philosophy for a generation navigating housing they cannot afford, careers without clear ladders, and a future routinely described in catastrophic terms.

The word's rise sits squarely at the intersection of internet culture and popular psychology. It functions as a folk-theory cousin to "manifesting" and the older "fake it till you make it," repackaging a genuine cognitive phenomenon — what psychologists call *positive illusions* or *optimism bias* — into a single, shareable adjective. Researchers have long noted that mildly inflated self-beliefs can buffer against anxiety and sustain motivation; *delulu* names that gamble in the language of the feed.

Yet the term carries a built-in shrug, an awareness of its own absurdity. To call yourself delulu is to admit you may be wrong while choosing to proceed anyway. That ironic distance is precisely why it resonates in 2026: it lets people perform hope without the vulnerability of sincerely claiming it. In an era saturated with both relentless self-optimization and relentless doom, *delulu* offers a third path — a knowing, slightly ridiculous, defiantly cheerful refusal to be realistic.

### Related Words

- **manifesting** — the belief that focused positive thought can summon a desired reality; *delulu*'s earnest older sibling.
- **copium** — the comforting denial one inhales to cope with an unwanted truth; what the delulu may be running on.
- **optimism bias** — the well-documented tendency to overestimate the likelihood of good outcomes; the clinical engine beneath the slang.
