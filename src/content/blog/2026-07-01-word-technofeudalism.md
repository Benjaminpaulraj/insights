## Technofeudalism /ˌtɛk.noʊˈfjuː.də.lɪ.zəm/

**Part of speech:** noun

**Etymology:** Compound of Greek *technē* (art, craft, skill) and Medieval Latin *feudalismus*, from *feudum* (fief, estate granted in exchange for service). The portmanteau was notably advanced by Greek economist and former finance minister Yanis Varoufakis in his 2023 book *Technofeudalism: What Killed Capitalism*, though related concepts appeared in academic discourse as early as 2015.

---

### Definitions

1. *(economics / political theory)* An economic system in which digital platforms function as modern-day fiefdoms, with Big Tech corporations acting as lords who extract rent from "cloud serfs" — users, merchants, and producers who depend on those platforms to participate in the economy.
2. *(sociology)* The structural arrangement in which algorithmic systems, rather than market forces or democratic institutions, govern the allocation of economic opportunity, social visibility, and cultural access.
3. *(informal)* The condition of being economically dependent on — and unable to exit from — a digital platform ecosystem controlled by a single dominant actor.

### Usage in a Sentence

> "Critics argue that the subscription economy, where artists must continuously pay algorithmic tribute to streaming platforms to remain visible, is the most vivid expression of *technofeudalism* in everyday life."

> "The small business owner running her shop entirely within a platform's marketplace realized she had traded one landlord for another — this time, the rent was her data, her customer relationships, and a 30% transaction cut."

### Why This Word Is Everywhere Right Now

The concept of *technofeudalism* gained mainstream traction following Varoufakis's 2023 book, but it has fully entered the political vocabulary as AI-powered platforms have consolidated further through 2025 and into 2026. When a handful of companies control the infrastructure through which most of the world's commerce, communication, and creative work flows, the feudal metaphor stops feeling like an exaggeration. The "cloud" is not neutral infrastructure — it is owned land, and access to it comes at a price set by the landowner.

What distinguishes technofeudalism from ordinary monopoly capitalism is the rent dynamic. Traditional capitalism involves the sale of goods and services; under technofeudalism, the platform does not sell a product so much as it taxes participation. A seller on a major e-commerce platform does not merely pay for a service — she is a "cloud vassal" who tills the lord's land, generates value, and remits a portion upward. The data she produces, the audience she builds, the reviews she accumulates — none of it is truly hers if the platform can revoke access overnight.

The rise of generative AI has intensified this discourse sharply. AI models were trained on content produced by millions of workers, writers, and creators without compensation — an enclosure of the digital commons reminiscent of the original feudal land grabs of the 15th and 16th centuries. As AI systems absorb ever more economic value at the top of the stack, and as workers increasingly produce content that feeds these models without sharing in the returns, the structural analogy becomes harder to dismiss as mere academic rhetoric. In 2026, *technofeudalism* is less a critique and more a description.

### Related Words

- **Rentierism** — the economic practice of deriving income from ownership of assets rather than productive labor; the classical precursor to the technofeudal model
- **Platform capitalism** — a related term emphasizing how platforms extract value from network effects rather than production; more common in academic sociology
- **Cloud serfdom** — informal term for the condition of digital dependency, particularly for gig-economy workers and creators whose livelihoods are entirely platform-mediated
