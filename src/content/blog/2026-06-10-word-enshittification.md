## Enshittification /ɛnˌʃɪt.ɪ.fɪˈkeɪ.ʃən/

**Part of speech:** noun

**Etymology:** English coinage, formed from the intensifying prefix *en-* (meaning "to put into" or "to cause to become"), the vulgar root *shit*, and the nominalizing suffix *-fication* ("the process of making"). Coined by writer Cory Doctorow in late 2022 and popularized through 2023; named the American Dialect Society's Word of the Year for 2023.

---

### Definitions

1. *(primary meaning)* The gradual deterioration of an online platform or digital service, in which quality is systematically degraded over time to extract value from users and business partners for the benefit of shareholders.
2. *(by extension)* The general pattern by which any product, institution, or service declines in usefulness once its provider has captured a dependent audience.
3. *(informal)* The lived feeling that the apps, websites, and tools one relies on are quietly getting worse, more cluttered, and more hostile.

---

### Usage in a Sentence

> "The search engine's enshittification was complete the day organic results vanished beneath three screens of advertisements."

> "She blamed the app's sudden enshittification on the new owners, who had loaded every feature behind a paywall."

---

### Why This Word Is Everywhere Right Now

Enshittification has graduated from a single blogger's provocation into shorthand for a structural grievance about the digital economy. Doctorow's original formulation described a predictable three-act decline: platforms are first good to their users to build a base, then abuse those users to court business customers, then abuse the business customers to claw back value for themselves — until little usable remains. The word resonates because it gives a precise name to an experience nearly everyone now shares: the slow, deliberate worsening of services that once felt indispensable.

Its persistence into 2026 reflects a hardening mood. As more of daily life runs through a handful of dominant platforms — for search, shopping, messaging, transport, and increasingly AI assistance — the question of *why everything feels worse* has moved from tech criticism into mainstream economics and policy. Regulators cite the dynamic in antitrust arguments; journalists invoke it to explain everything from streaming-service price creep to the flooding of feeds with low-quality, algorithmically optimized content. The term endures precisely because it frames decline not as accident or incompetence but as a rational, profit-driven strategy.

What makes the word culturally potent is that it converts a diffuse frustration into a thesis. To call something "enshittified" is not merely to complain that it is bad, but to assert that it was *made* bad on purpose, by identifiable incentives. That analytical edge — vulgar yet rigorous — is why the term has crossed from internet subcultures into boardrooms, op-eds, and ordinary conversation.

---

### Related Words

- **Platform decay** — the more clinical, polite synonym favored in academic and policy writing.
- **Rent-seeking** — the economic concept of extracting value without creating it, which underpins the mechanism.
- **Lock-in** — the dependence that makes enshittification possible, by raising the cost of leaving a worsening service.
