## Vibe Coding /ˈvaɪb ˌkoʊdɪŋ/

**Part of speech:** noun (also used attributively); occasionally verb (*to vibe-code*)

**Etymology:** A compound neologism, English, coined early 2025. From *vibe* (slang, 1960s, a clipping of *vibration* in the sense of an intuitive feeling or atmosphere) + *coding* (the writing of computer instructions, from Latin *codex*, "book, code of laws"). Popularized in February 2025 by a widely shared remark from an AI researcher describing a style of programming where one "fully gives in to the vibes" and lets a language model do the typing.

---

### Definitions

1. *(primary meaning)* A method of building software in which a person describes desired behavior in ordinary language to an AI model, accepts the generated code largely on intuition, and iterates by conversation rather than by reading or writing the code directly.
2. *(by extension)* An approach to any technical task in which results are guided by feel and rapid trial-and-error with an AI assistant, rather than by deep underlying expertise.
3. *(informal, sometimes derisive)* The practice of shipping code one does not fully understand because it appears to work.

---

### Usage in a Sentence

> "She vibe-coded a working prototype of the booking app over a weekend, despite never having written a line of JavaScript."

> "The outage was traced to a vibe-coded module that no one on the team could actually explain."

---

### Why This Word Is Everywhere Right Now

What began as an offhand description of a personal workflow has, in little more than a year, become the defining phrase of a shift in how software is made. As large language models grew fluent enough to generate, debug, and refactor whole programs from plain instructions, the bottleneck of building moved away from syntax and toward intention. *Vibe coding* names that change: the act of programming by describing, not typing. The phrase spread because it captured something practitioners already felt — that the craft had quietly changed underneath them — and gave it a handle catchy enough to argue about.

And argue people did. To its champions, vibe coding is a democratization story, lowering the barrier to building so that founders, designers, scientists, and hobbyists can turn ideas into working tools without years of training. To its critics, it is a quiet erosion of understanding — software accreting in layers that no human has read, with bugs, security holes, and brittle assumptions hidden inside confident-looking output. The word now carries both meanings at once, admiring and wary, depending on who says it. That tension is exactly why it has stuck.

Beneath the slang sits a genuinely open question about expertise. If a model can produce the code, what is it that a programmer actually knows? The debate has spilled out of engineering teams and into classrooms, hiring conversations, and boardrooms weighing how much of their work to entrust to systems they cannot fully audit. Vibe coding is everywhere right now because it is shorthand for a larger unease and a larger excitement — about how much we are willing to delegate to machines, and how comfortable we are building on things we no longer entirely comprehend.
