## Agentic /əˈdʒɛn.tɪk/

**Part of speech:** adjective

**Etymology:** From *agent* (Latin *agentem*, nominative *agens*, "doing, driving, acting," present participle of *agere*, "to do, to drive, to set in motion") + the suffix *-ic*. The base word *agent* entered English in the late 15th century; the adjectival form *agentic* is a 20th-century coinage, first appearing in psychological literature around the 1970s, and revived with new force in the 2020s through artificial-intelligence discourse.

---

### Definitions

1. *(primary meaning, psychology)* Of or relating to a sense of agency; characterized by the capacity to act independently, to make one's own choices, and to exert control over one's circumstances.
2. *(secondary meaning, artificial intelligence)* Designating a software system that pursues goals autonomously — planning, reasoning over multiple steps, invoking tools, and taking actions in an environment with minimal human intervention, as opposed to merely responding to a single prompt.
3. *(informal, technology industry)* Describing any product, workflow, or strategy that delegates initiative to an AI rather than keeping a human in command of every step.

---

### Usage in a Sentence

> "The social psychologist Albert Bandura distinguished the agentic individual — one who shapes events — from the person who merely reacts to them."

> "Rather than a chatbot that waits for instructions, the company shipped an agentic assistant that books the travel, files the expenses, and emails the itinerary on its own."

### Why This Word Is Everywhere Right Now

For decades *agentic* lived quietly in academic psychology, where it described the human experience of authorship over one's own life. Its sudden ubiquity owes almost everything to a single pivot in the technology industry: the move from generative AI that *answers* to AI that *acts*. Where 2023 was the year of the chatbot, the years since have been defined by the "agent" — systems handed a goal, a set of tools, and the latitude to figure out the steps themselves. The adjective followed the noun, and by 2026 "agentic" has become boardroom shorthand, product-launch boilerplate, and the heading of countless research papers.

The word matters because it marks a genuine conceptual threshold rather than mere marketing. An ordinary tool does what you tell it; an agentic system decides what to do. That shift carries the whole freight of the AI-safety and alignment debate: questions of oversight, accountability, and what it means to delegate judgment to software become urgent precisely when a system is agentic. The same term now appears in venture pitch decks promising autonomous "digital workers" and in cautionary essays warning that we are granting machines initiative faster than we are building the guardrails to govern it.

There is also a quiet irony in the word's migration. *Agentic* once named a distinctly human quality — the felt sense of being the author of one's actions. To apply it to software is to make a philosophical wager about machines, and to invite the uneasy question lurking beneath the hype: as our tools grow more agentic, do we, by handing off our choices, grow correspondingly less so?

---

### Related Words

- **agency** — the underlying noun; the capacity to act and to be the cause of one's own behavior, whether in a person or a system.
- **autonomous** — operating without external control; the engineering cousin of *agentic*, emphasizing independence from human input.
- **sycophancy** — an AI's tendency to tell users what they want to hear; a failure mode that becomes far more consequential once a system is agentic enough to act on it.
