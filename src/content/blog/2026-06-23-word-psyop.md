## Psyop /ˈsaɪˌɒp/

**Part of speech:** noun (also attributive)

**Etymology:** Clipping of *psychological operation*, a compound coined in mid-20th-century Anglophone military doctrine (attested from the 1940s–50s) from Greek-derived *psycho-* ("of the mind," from *psūkhḗ*, "soul, breath") and Latin *operatio* ("a working, performance"). The shortened form *psyop* enters general slang in the 2010s and becomes ubiquitous online by the early 2020s.

---

### Definitions

1. *(primary, military / intelligence)* A planned operation intended to influence the emotions, attitudes, beliefs, or behavior of a target audience, typically through the controlled dissemination of selected information.
2. *(secondary, by extension)* Any coordinated effort — commercial, political, or institutional — believed to manipulate public perception by covert or deceptive means.
3. *(informal / colloquial)* A jocular or paranoid accusation that some widely held belief, trend, product, or even one's own feeling is artificial and deliberately engineered; often hyperbolic, as in *"pumpkin spice season is a psyop."*

---

### Usage in a Sentence

> "Declassified records confirm the leaflets were part of a wartime psyop designed to lower enemy morale."

> "He's convinced that the sudden popularity of the song is a record-label psyop, but honestly it's just catchy."

### Why This Word Is Everywhere Right Now

The migration of *psyop* from a NATO field manual to a punchline tells you something about how people now experience information. A term once reserved for clandestine state influence campaigns has become the default vocabulary for a population that assumes, almost by reflex, that nothing it encounters is fully organic. When every feed is algorithmically curated, every trend is potentially astroturfed, and every product launch is wrapped in engineered hype, "is this a psyop?" stops being conspiracy and starts feeling like media literacy.

Part of the word's appeal is its elasticity. In its ironic register — "the gym being crowded in January is a psyop" — it lets people name a real, low-grade suspicion without committing to a real conspiracy theory. It is a way of gesturing at manipulation while laughing at oneself for noticing. But the joke rests on a genuine erosion of trust. In an environment of generative AI, coordinated inauthentic behavior, and opaque recommendation systems, the line between an actual influence operation and ordinary marketing has genuinely blurred, and the slang tracks that blur with uncanny precision.

There is also a darker fold. The more *psyop* is invoked, the easier it becomes to dismiss inconvenient facts as manufactured — to treat skepticism as a posture rather than a practice. A word that began as a description of deception now risks becoming a tool for it, letting anyone wave away evidence as just another operation. That dual life — sharp media criticism and lazy thought-terminating reflex living in the same five letters — is exactly why the term feels so native to this moment.

### Related Words

- **astroturfing** — manufacturing the appearance of grassroots support; a psyop's commercial cousin.
- **gaslighting** — manipulating someone into doubting their own perception, the interpersonal-scale version of the same anxiety.
- **manufactured consent** — Herman and Chomsky's framework for how media shapes public opinion; the theory beneath the meme.
