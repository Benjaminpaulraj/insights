## Slop /slɒp/

**Part of speech:** noun (also verb)

**Etymology:** From Middle English *sloppe* ("mud, muck, slush, a muddy place"), likely related to Old English *-sloppe* (as in *cūsloppe*, "cowslip," literally "cow-slobber") and ultimately to the root of *slip*. Recorded from the 14th century for spilled liquid or semi-liquid mess; the sense of "unappetizing, watery food" emerged by the 19th century. The contemporary technological sense — low-quality, mass-produced AI content — entered wide usage around 2024.

---

### Definitions

1. *(primary, contemporary)* Low-quality digital content — text, images, audio, or video — generated by artificial intelligence in bulk and published with little or no human oversight, typically to attract clicks, game algorithms, or fill space.
2. *(traditional)* Watery food waste or kitchen refuse, especially that fed to pigs.
3. *(traditional)* Spilled or carelessly handled liquid; soft mud or slush.
4. *(verb)* To spill or splash a liquid clumsily; figuratively, to flood a channel with such low-grade content.

---

### Usage in a Sentence

> "Her search results used to be useful, but lately the first page is just slop — three near-identical articles that say nothing, each topped with a six-fingered stock illustration."

> "We can't let the comments section turn into slop; readers can tell when a 'reply' was never written by a person."

---

### Why This Word Is Everywhere Right Now

The genius of *slop* is that it arrived already fully formed. We did not have to invent a new term for the deluge of synthetic content washing across the internet — we simply reached for the oldest word we had for cheap, watery, machine-fed mush and pointed it at our screens. By analogy with *spam*, which named unwanted email by way of a canned-meat punchline, *slop* names unwanted AI output by evoking the trough. The metaphor does the moral work: it tells you the stuff is abundant, formless, and not really meant for you to enjoy.

Its rise tracks a real shift. As generative models made it nearly free to produce plausible-looking articles, images, and videos, the economics of publishing inverted. Where once content was expensive and attention cheap, now content is nearly infinite and human attention is the scarce resource being strip-mined. Search engines, social feeds, and even academic and book-selling platforms have visibly degraded under the volume, prompting a wave of "is the internet dead?" anxiety and a renewed premium on anything verifiably human-made. Calling it *slop* is partly a defense mechanism — a way of reasserting taste in a landscape engineered to erode it.

What makes the word stick is that it captures a feeling, not just a category. You often cannot prove a given image or paragraph is AI-generated, but you can sense the sloppiness: the uncanny smoothness, the confident emptiness, the way it pattern-matches the shape of meaning without its substance. *Slop* gives that suspicion a name, and in doing so turns a private unease into a shared cultural judgment — a small act of resistance, repeated millions of times a day, against being fed.

### Related Words

- **spam** — the linguistic ancestor; unwanted bulk content, named with the same instinct to dismiss it as junk food.
- **enshittification** — the gradual decay of a platform's quality as it prioritizes profit over users; slop is often the visible residue of this process.
- **hallucination** — when an AI confidently fabricates information; slop is frequently hallucination at scale, dressed up as authority.
- **doomscrolling** — the compulsive consumption of bleak feeds; increasingly, what one doomscrolls through is slop.
