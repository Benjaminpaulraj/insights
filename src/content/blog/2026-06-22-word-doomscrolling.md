## Doomscrolling /ˈduːmˌskroʊlɪŋ/

**Part of speech:** noun; also *doomscroll* (verb)

**Etymology:** A compound of *doom* (Old English *dōm*, "judgment, fate, ruin") and *scroll* (from the verb sense of moving text on a screen, itself from Old French *escroe*, "strip of parchment"). The blend emerged in English around 2018–2020, gaining mainstream traction during the COVID-19 pandemic.

---

### Definitions

1. *(noun)* The act of continuing to consume a stream of distressing or negative news online, often for an extended period, despite the discomfort it causes.
2. *(verb)* To scroll compulsively through bad news, frequently late at night or during moments of anxiety, in a self-perpetuating cycle.
3. *(informal)* Any prolonged, restless session of screen-reading that leaves one feeling worse than before it began.

### Usage in a Sentence

> "She meant to check the weather, but an hour later she was still doomscrolling through reports of a crisis on the other side of the world."

> "Sleep researchers warn that doomscrolling before bed disrupts the very rest we need to cope with the news we cannot stop reading."

### Why This Word Is Everywhere Right Now

Doomscrolling names a behavior that feels almost universally recognizable in the age of the infinite feed. Its rise tracks neatly with the architecture of modern platforms: endless scroll, algorithmic amplification of outrage, and a steady drip of push notifications engineered to pull attention back. Psychologists link the habit to **negativity bias** — the evolutionary tendency to weight threatening information more heavily than reassuring information. What once helped our ancestors survive now keeps us thumbing past one alarming headline to the next, searching for a sense of control that never quite arrives.

The word matters because it gives a name to a quietly corrosive form of digital distress. Researchers have associated heavy doomscrolling with elevated anxiety, disrupted sleep, and symptoms of secondary trauma, particularly during periods of political turbulence, public-health emergencies, and climate reporting. Naming the behavior is the first step toward interrupting it — and the term has become shorthand in conversations about "news avoidance," digital wellbeing, and the deliberate design of attention economies.

In recent years, doomscrolling has also become a cultural mirror. It captures the paradox of being more informed than any generation in history while feeling more helpless. The word endures not because the news is uniquely bad, but because the *scroll* is uniquely bottomless — and we are still learning, collectively, how to look away.

---

### Related Words

- **Negativity bias** — the cognitive tendency to give greater psychological weight to negative experiences than positive ones.
- **News avoidance** — the conscious decision to limit or disconnect from news, often a reaction against doomscrolling.
- **Brainrot** — the perceived mental dulling that follows excessive low-quality online consumption.
