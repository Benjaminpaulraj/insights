Word of the Day

Brainrot

/ˈbreɪn.rɒt/
noun · also attributive
7 June 2026 culture social media mental health psychology language
Read
Etymology

A closed compound of English brain (Old English brægen, "the organ of thought") and rot (Old English rotian, "to decay, decompose"). The literal pairing appears as early as the 19th century — Henry David Thoreau used it in Walden (1854) to lament intellectual decline — but its present sense emerged on the internet in the early 2020s and was named Oxford University Press's Word of the Year in 2024.

Definitions
noun (also attributive, as in brainrot humor)
  1. (primary meaning) The supposed deterioration of a person's mental or intellectual faculties, especially as a result of overconsuming trivial, low-quality, or algorithmically served online content.
  2. (secondary meaning) The genre of such content itself — absurd, hyper-stimulating, often deliberately nonsensical media designed for endless scrolling.
  3. (informal / colloquial usage) A state of being so immersed in a particular topic, fandom, or meme that one can think of little else: "I've got total Roman Empire brainrot."
Usage in a Sentence
"After three hours of short-form videos, she felt the familiar fog of brainrot settle in — eyes glazed, attention shot."
"Half the slang my students use is pure brainrot; I had to look up four words just to grade their group chat."
Why This Word Is Everywhere Right Now

Brainrot won its moment because it gave a name to a feeling almost everyone now recognizes. The infinite scroll — engineered to maximize time-on-app — has made the sensation of mental erosion a daily, shared experience. When Oxford crowned it Word of the Year in 2024, the choice was less a prediction than a diagnosis: a culture describing, in real time, what it suspects its own tools are doing to it.

What makes the term unusually sticky is its self-awareness. The same online communities that produce the most aggressively meaningless content also coined the word to mock it. Brainrot is simultaneously an accusation and a badge — people complain about it while gleefully tagging their own posts with it. That irony mirrors a genuine ambivalence: we know the attention economy is extracting something from us, yet we keep paying.

Researchers in psychology and neuroscience have leaned into the conversation, studying how fragmented, high-novelty media may reshape attention spans, working memory, and the brain's reward circuitry. Whether "brainrot" describes a measurable cognitive effect or simply a cultural mood remains contested — but the word's explosive uptake is itself evidence of how seriously a generation takes the question.

Related Words
doomscrolling the compulsive consumption of distressing news; a frequent on-ramp to brainrot.
enshittification Cory Doctorow's term for the gradual decay of online platforms — the structural cousin of brainrot's personal decay.
dopamine the neurotransmitter most often invoked to explain why the scroll is so hard to stop.
